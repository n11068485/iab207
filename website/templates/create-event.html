Acknowledgement of Country
Acknowledgement of Country

An Acknowledgement of Country is a statement that recognises the ongoing connection Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have to their traditional lands, waters and communities. It is typically made at the start of a meeting, event, or gathering and can be delivered by anyone — Indigenous or non-Indigenous.

An Acknowledgement is a genuine act of respect that contributes to reconciliation. It signals that your event recognises the First Peoples of Australia as the original and ongoing Custodians of the land — custodians of one of the world's oldest continuous living cultures.

Welcome to Country — what's the difference?

A Welcome to Country is a formal ceremony that can only be performed by an authorised Traditional Custodian (or their representative) of the specific land where the event is held. It grants permission for visitors to be on Country.

The two are not interchangeable. If a Welcome to Country is performed by an authorised Custodian, a separate Acknowledgement is not required — but an Acknowledgement alone can never replace a Welcome to Country.

Why it matters: Australia's First Nations peoples hold the longest continuing cultures in human history. Acknowledging Country at public events is one step toward genuine reconciliation — recognising prior and ongoing sovereignty, and building a more respectful and equitable society.

Authoritative Resources for Identifying Traditional Custodians
  • AIATSIS Map of Indigenous Australia — The most comprehensive publicly available map of First Nations language groups and Country boundaries.
    aiatsis.gov.au/explore/map-indigenous-australia
  • Reconciliation Australia — Acknowledgement of Country — Official guidance from Australia's peak reconciliation body, including sample wordings and best-practice advice.
    reconciliation.org.au/our-work/education/acknowledgement-of-country
  • Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (AIATSIS) — The national institution preserving and promoting First Nations culture, history and heritage.
    aiatsis.gov.au
  • First Languages Australia — Supports maintenance and revival of First Nations languages; useful for understanding Country and nation names.
    firstlanguages.org.au
  • State and Territory Government Protocols — Each government publishes Acknowledgement of Country protocols reflecting local knowledge and community preferences. Search "[your state] government Acknowledgement of Country protocol".
Best practice: Where possible, contact the local Land Council or Aboriginal Community Controlled Organisation directly to confirm the correct name and preferred wording for your location.
Common Mistakes and Contested Issues to Be Aware Of
  • Tokenism: Reciting an acknowledgement quickly and insincerely — just for form's sake — is disrespectful and undermines the practice. Deliver it meaningfully.
  • Wrong Country name: Using the incorrect Nation or Country name for your event location is a significant error. Always verify before publishing.
  • Confusing AoC with Welcome to Country: Only an authorised Traditional Custodian can perform a Welcome to Country. An Acknowledgement is not a substitute.
  • Vague language: Referring only to "the Traditional Custodians" without naming them is considered insufficient for an enhanced acknowledgement.
  • Contested phrasing: Phrases such as "sovereignty was never ceded" are politically significant — use them only with genuine understanding and intent, not as a slogan.
  • Mispronunciation: Take the time to learn correct pronunciation of Country and peoples' names before delivering publicly.
  • Overlooking multiple groups: Many locations have multiple overlapping Custodian groups — acknowledge all relevant peoples, not just one.
  • Copy-pasting without thought: Copying another event's acknowledgement without checking its accuracy for your specific location can result in errors.
